if the expansion had been at a much higher rate, all of the matter would have thinned out before galaxies could form. if everything were expanding at a much slower rate, then gravity would have “won.” the expansion would have reversed and all of the matter would have recollapsed, probably into a black hole—again, no stars, no planets, no life. the development of life on earth depends on still-luckier coincidences. had matter and antimatter been present initially in exactly equal proportions, then all matter would have been annihilated and turned into pure energy. we owe our existence to the fact that there was slightly more matter than antimatter. (after most of the matter made contact with an equal amount of antimatter, turning into energy, a small amount of additional matter must have been present. we are all descendants of that bit of “unbalanced” matter.) if nuclear fusion reactions occurred at a somewhat faster rate than they actually do, then at the time of the initial fireball, all of the matter would have been converted from hydrogen into helium into carbon and all the way into iron (the most stable nucleus).
