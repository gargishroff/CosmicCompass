3.3 newton’s universal law of gravitation gravity, the attractive force between all masses, is what keeps the planets in orbit. newton’s universal law of gravitation relates the gravitational force to mass and distance: the force of gravity is what gives us our sense of weight. unlike mass, which is constant, weight can vary depending on the force of gravity (or acceleration) you feel. when kepler’s laws are reexamined in the light of newton’s gravitational law, it becomes clear that the masses of both objects are important for the third law, which becomes a3 = (m1 + m2) × p2. mutual gravitational effects permit us to calculate the masses of astronomical objects, from comets to galaxies. 3.4 orbits in the solar system the closest point in a satellite orbit around earth is its perigee, and the farthest point is its apogee (corresponding to perihelion and aphelion for an orbit around the sun). the planets follow orbits around the sun that are nearly circular and in the same plane. most asteroids are found between mars and jupiter in the asteroid belt, whereas comets generally follow orbits of high eccentricity.
