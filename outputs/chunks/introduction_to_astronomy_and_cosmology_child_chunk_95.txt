(in other words, the greater the temperature, the shorter the peak wavelength.) the peak wavelength, λmax, is thus given by a constant, wien’s constant, divided by the temperature. it value is 2.897 ! 10"3 m k so with λ in metres and t in kelvin: λmax # 2.897 ! 10"3/t or, with λ in nanometres: λmax # 2.897 ! 106/t wien’s law enables the temperature of inaccessible objects, such as a lava ﬂ ow or the interior of a blast furnace (an excellent black body as it really is a cavity), to be measured and, as we will see, allows us to estimate the surface temperature of the sun.
