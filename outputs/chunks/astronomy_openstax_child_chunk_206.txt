we know eight planets, beginning with mercury closest to the sun and extending outward to neptune. the average orbital data for the planets are summarized in table 3.2. (ceres is the largest of the asteroids, now considered a dwarf planet.) according to kepler’s laws, mercury must have the shortest orbital period (88 earth-days); thus, it has the highest orbital speed, averaging 48 kilometers per second. at the opposite extreme, neptune has a period of 165 years and an average orbital speed of just 5 kilometers per second. all the planets have orbits of rather low eccentricity. the most eccentric orbit is that of mercury (0.21); the rest have eccentricities smaller than 0.1. it is fortunate that among the rest, mars has an eccentricity greater than that of many of the other planets. otherwise the pre-telescopic observations of brahe would not have been sufficient for kepler to deduce that its orbit had the shape of an ellipse rather than a circle. the planetary orbits are also confined close to a common plane, which is near the plane of earth’s orbit (called the ecliptic).
