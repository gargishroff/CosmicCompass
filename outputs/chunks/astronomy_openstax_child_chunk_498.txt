the longer the waves, the harder it is to resolve fine detail in the images or maps we make. because radio waves have such long wavelengths, they present tremendous challenges for astronomers who need good resolution. in fact, even the largest radio dishes on earth, operating alone, cannot make out as much detail as the typical small visible-light telescope used in a college astronomy lab. to overcome this difficulty, radio astronomers have learned to sharpen their images by linking two or more radio telescopes together electronically. two or more telescopes linked together in this way are called an interferometer. “interferometer” may seem like a strange term because the telescopes in an interferometer work cooperatively; they don’t “interfere” with each other. interference, however, is a technical term for the way that multiple waves interact with each other when they arrive in our instruments, and this interaction allows us to coax more detail out of our observations. the resolution of an interferometer depends upon the separation of the telescopes, not upon their individual apertures.
