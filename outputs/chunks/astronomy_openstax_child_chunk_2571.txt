to describe just how space stretches, we say the cosmic expansion causes the universe to undergo a uniform change in scale over time. by scale we mean, for example, the distance between two clusters of galaxies. it is customary to represent the scale by the factor r; if r doubles, then the distance between the clusters has doubled. since the universe is expanding at the same rate everywhere, the change in r tells us how much it has expanded (or contracted) at any given time. for a static universe, r would be constant as time passes. in an expanding universe, r increases with time. if it is space that is stretching rather than galaxies moving through space, then why do the galaxies show redshifts in their spectra? when you were young and naïve—a few chapters ago—it was fine to discuss the redshifts of distant galaxies as if they resulted from their motion away from us. but now that you are an older and wiser student of cosmology, this view will simply not do. a more accurate view of the redshifts of galaxies is that the light waves are stretched by the stretching of the space they travel through. think about the light from a remote galaxy. as it moves away from its source, the light has to travel through space.
