his growing reputation gained him the patronage of the danish king frederick ii, and at the age of 30, brahe was able to establish a fine astronomical observatory on the north sea island of hven (figure 3.2). brahe was the last and greatest of the pre-telescopic observers in europe. figure 3.2 tycho brahe (1546–1601) and johannes kepler (1571–1630). (a) a stylized engraving shows tycho brahe using his instruments to measure the altitude of celestial objects above the horizon. the large curved instrument in the foreground allowed him to measure precise angles in the sky. note that the scene includes hints of the grandeur of brahe’s observatory at hven. (b) kepler was a german mathematician and astronomer. his discovery of the basic laws that describe planetary motion placed the heliocentric cosmology of copernicus on a firm mathematical basis. at hven, brahe made a continuous record of the positions of the sun, moon, and planets for almost 20 years.
