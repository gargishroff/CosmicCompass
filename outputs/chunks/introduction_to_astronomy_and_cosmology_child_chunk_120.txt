we are, in fact, just seeing down through the solar atmosphere to a depth where the gas becomes what is called ‘optically thick’. this deepest visible layer of the atmosphere is called the photosphere (as this is where the photons that we see originate from) and is about 500 km thick (figure 2.8). the temperature falls from ∼6500 k at its base to ∼4400 k at its upper region. as was derived earlier, the effective temperature of the photosphere is ∼5800 k. the convective transport of energy from below gives rise to a mottling of the surface – solar granulations that are about 1000 km across. each granulation cell last about 5–10 min as hot gas, having risen from below the surface radiates energy away, cools and sinks down again. figure 2.8 the photosphere of the sun showing granulations and sunspot groups. image: soho space observatory, nasa, esa.
