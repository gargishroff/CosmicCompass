it may, of course, survive its initial passage near the sun and return to the cold reaches of space where it spent the previous 4.5 billion years. at the other extreme, it may collide with the sun or come so close that it is destroyed on its first perihelion passage (several such collisions have been observed with space telescopes that monitor the sun). sometimes, however, the new comet does not come that close to the sun but instead interacts with one or more of the planets. soho (the solar and heliospheric observatory) has an excellent collection of videos of comets (https://openstax.org/l/30sohocomvid) that come near the sun. at this site, comet ison approaches the sun and is believed to be destroyed in its passage. a comet that comes within the gravitational influence of a planet has three possible fates. it can (1) impact the planet, ending the story at once; (2) speed up and be ejected, leaving the solar system forever; or (3) be perturbed into an orbit with a shorter period. in the last case, its fate is sealed.
