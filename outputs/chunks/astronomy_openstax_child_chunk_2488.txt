one of the major goals of this telescope is to observe directly the light of the first galaxies and even the first stars to shine. the most distant galaxy observed with it, as of this writing, was already formed a mere 330 million years after the big bang. the atacama large millimeter array (alma) in chile has detected far-infrared and millimeter-wave radiation from massive star-bursting galaxies and has also measured redshifts that show that these galaxies were already in existence only a few hundred million years after the expansion of the universe began. (note these young galaxies are being detected at infrared and millimeter wavelengths because their high redshifts shift the electromagnetic radiation that they emit to very long wavelengths.) astronomers were initially surprised to find that galaxies could form so quickly, and they are now exploring new models that incorporate ordinary matter, dark matter with various properties, and even massive primordial black holes that formed soon after the big bang, to explain the rapid, early formation of galaxies. at this point, if you have been thinking about our discussions of the expanding universe in galaxies, you may be wondering what exactly in figure 28.21 is expanding.
