(remember that this compression will not be halted by another type of fusion in these low-mass stars.) the whole star follows along, shrinking and also becoming very hot—reaching surface temperatures as high as 100,000 k. such hot stars are very strong sources of stellar winds and ultraviolet radiation, which sweep outward into the shells of material ejected when the star was a red giant. the winds and the ultraviolet radiation heat the shells, ionize them, and set them aglow (just as ultraviolet radiation from hot, young stars produces h ii regions; see between the stars: gas and dust in space).
