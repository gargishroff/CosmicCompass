launched in 1997, cassini arrived in 2004 and went into orbit around saturn, beginning extensive studies of its rings and moons, as well as the planet itself. in january 2005, cassini deployed an entry probe into the atmosphere of saturn’s large moon, titan, where it successfully landed on the surface. (we’ll discuss the probe and what it found in the chapter on rings, moons, and pluto.) the voyager and galileo missions to jupiter were primarily designed to study the moons and the atmosphere of the planet. the next nasa mission, an orbiter called juno, arrived at jupiter in july 2016. in order to meet its objectives of studying the jovian magnetosphere, it has a very elongated (eccentric) 55-day orbit, that takes it from 4 thousand kilometers above the cloud tops out to 76 thousand kilometers. the orbit takes the craft over jupiter’s poles, giving us remarkable close-ups of the polar regions (previous spacecraft viewed the planet from lower latitudes). juno was originally designed without a camera, but fortunately scientists rectified this omission, adding a simple downward-looking color camera to use during close passes by jupiter.
