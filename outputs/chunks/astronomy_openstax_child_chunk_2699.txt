in a series of experiments known as the miller-urey experiments, pioneered by stanley miller and harold urey at 30.2 • astrobiology 1059 [image: astronomy_openstax_page1077_img1.jpeg] the university of chicago, biochemists have simulated conditions on early earth and have been able to produce some of the fundamental building blocks of life, including those that form proteins and other large biological molecules known as nucleic acids (which we will discuss shortly). although these experiments produced encouraging results, there are some problems with them. the most interesting chemistry from a biological perspective takes place with hydrogen-rich or reducing gases, such as ammonia and methane. however, the early atmosphere of earth was probably dominated by carbon dioxide (as venus’ and mars’ atmospheres still are today) and may not have contained an abundance of reducing gases comparable to that used in miller-urey type experiments. hydrothermal vents—seafloor systems in which ocean water is superheated and circulated through crustal or mantle rocks before reemerging into the ocean—have also been suggested as potential contributors of organic compounds on the early earth, and such sources would not require earth to have an early reducing atmosphere.
