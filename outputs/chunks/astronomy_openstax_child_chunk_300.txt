the rotation of earth would carry an observer at any given place alternately into regions of deeper and shallower water. an observer being carried toward the regions under or opposite the moon, where the water was deepest, would say, “the tide is coming in”; when carried away from those regions, the observer would say, “the tide is going out.” during a day, the observer would be carried through two tidal bulges (one on each side of earth) and so would experience two high tides and two low tides. the sun also produces tides on earth, although it is less than half as effective as the moon at tide raising. the actual tides we experience are a combination of the larger effect of the moon and the smaller effect of the sun. when the sun and moon are lined up (at new moon or full moon), the tides produced reinforce each other link to learning 4.6 • ocean tides and the moon 123 [image: astronomy_openstax_page141_img1.jpeg] [image: astronomy_openstax_page141_img2.jpeg] and so are greater than normal (figure 4.19).
