helium, for example, is the second-most abundant element in the sun. helium has two protons in its nucleus instead of the single proton that characterizes hydrogen. in addition, the helium nucleus contains two neutrons, particles with a mass comparable to that of the proton but with no electric charge. moving around this nucleus are two electrons, so the total net charge of the helium atom is also zero (figure 5.16). figure 5.16 helium atom. here we see a schematic diagram of a helium atom in its lowest energy state. two protons are present in the nucleus of all helium atoms. in the most common variety of helium, the nucleus also contains two neutrons, which have nearly the same mass as the proton but carry no charge. two electrons orbit the nucleus. from this description of hydrogen and helium, perhaps you have guessed the pattern for building up all the elements (different types of atoms) that we find in the universe. the type of element is determined by the number of protons in the nucleus of the atom. for example, any atom with six protons is the element carbon, with eight protons is oxygen, with 26 is iron, and with 92 is uranium.
